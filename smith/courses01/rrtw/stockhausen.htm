"The Greatest Work of Art in the Entire Cosmos"
Robert Hilferty

Robert Hilferty looks at Karlheinz Stockhausen's notorious statement about the terrorist attacks of 11 September.


The views represented in the following essay are those of the author; they do not necessarily reflect the opinions of andante or its staff or associates. andante's editors and the author are mindful of the gravity of recent events and of the sensitivities surrounding them, but, because of Karlheinz Stockhausen's stature as a composer, andante chose to publish this commentary on his controversial remarks concerning the attacks on the World Trade Center. Other points of view regarding this incident are welcome; please write to letters@andante.com.

 

Karlheinz Stockhausen is 73 years old. He may be coming down with Alzheimer's or Mad Cow Disease or maybe he's always had a screw loose — how else could he have been so wildly "experimental" over his brilliant, groundbreaking career? On 16 September the German composer made some inflammatory remarks to a group of journalists concerning the recent terrorist attacks on the World Trade Center and the Pentagon. He now finds his music subject to a potentially massive boycott, and many a journalist, musician and colleague is condemning him as much as the terrorists.

What exactly did the man say? Die Zeit quotes Stockhausen as follows:

Was da geschehen ist, ist — jetzt müssen Sie alle Ihr Gehirn umstellen — das größte Kunstwerk, das es je gegeben hat. Dass Geister in einem Akt etwas vollbringen, was wir in der Musik nicht träumen könnten, dass Leute zehn Jahre üben wie verrückt, total fanatisch für ein Konzert, und dann sterben. Das ist das größte Kunstwerk, das es überhaupt gibt für den ganzen Kosmos. Stellen Sie sich das doch vor, was da passiert ist. Da sind also Leute, die sind so konzentriert auf eine Aufführung, und dann werden 5000 Leute in die Auferstehung gejagt, in einem Moment. Das könnte ich nicht. Dagegen sind wir gar nichts als Komponisten. (...) Stellen Sie sich vor, ich könnte jetzt ein Kunstwerk schaffen und Sie wären alle nicht nur erstaunt, sondern Sie würden auf der Stelle umfallen, Sie wären tot und würden wiedergeboren, weil es einfach zu wahnsinnig ist. Manche Künstler versuchen doch auch über die Grenze des überhaupt Denkbaren und Möglichen zu gehen, damit wir wach werden, damit wir uns für eine andere Welt öffnen.

What has happened is — now you all have to turn your brains around — the greatest work of art there has ever been. That minds could achieve something in one act, which we in music cannot even dream of, that people rehearse like crazy for ten years, totally fanatically for one concert, and then die. This is the greatest possible work of art in the entire cosmos. Imagine what happened there. There are people who are so concentrated on one performance, and then 5000 people are chased into the Afterlife, in one moment. This I could not do. Compared to this, we are nothing as composers... Imagine this, that I could create a work of art now and you all were not only surprised, but you would fall down immediately, you would be dead and you would be reborn, because it is simply too insane. Some artists also try to cross the boundaries of what could ever be possible or imagined, to wake us up, to open another world for us.

It's understandable why Stockhausen miffed many. These remarks are over-the-top, and along the way he apparently expressed no sympathy for the victims. No standard (or, for that matter, avant-garde) definition of art includes killing a person, let alone thousands. No definition of art usually takes into account knocking down buildings or creating havoc. The idea of art conventionally revolves around notions of beauty, love, order and moral conscience — not ugliness, hate, chaos and evil. Fellow composer György Ligeti said of Stockhausen (as quoted in Der Spiegel): "If he interprets this treacherous mass murder as an artwork, then unfortunately I must say he ought to be confined to a psychiatric clinic."

Perhaps Stockhausen does belong in the loony bin, but he did preface his outrageous statement with the directive now you must all turn your brains around — indicating that, crazy or not, he was aware of proposing something out of the norm, even radical. (Under the circumstances, people weren't able to turn their brains around.) Attempts at damage control followed the outcry, whereupon Stockhausen, in a subsequent press conference, claimed that his statement was taken out of context, that his declaration revolved around the idea of Lucifer defined as "the cosmic spirit of rebellion, of anarchy. He uses his high degree of intelligence to destroy creation. He does not know love." If this is the case, his original statement is slightly easier to swallow. For the attacks would qualify as an awesome work of destruction. And there is a certain horrific beauty to destruction — just look at footage of the atom bomb mushrooming or old buildings imploding from dynamite or, on a cosmic scale, a star exploding. Moreover, there is something called "the art of war" which necessarily involves campaigns of death and destruction — and there was something artful to the war-like, deadly events of 11 September.

In fact, how could Stockhausen, who has been trying so hard over the years to create a "universal music" and a "cosmic music," not witness those horrific acts with a certain amount of envy and admiration? He was obviously impressed by the long-term planning, coordination and execution of this act by daring performers whose arena was not a tiny auditorium, but an entire city, if not a nation, and who not only hit their bigger-than-life, symbolically charged targets, but brought them down — knocking out America's two front teeth, so to speak — sacrificing themselves in the process. Talk about visceral, transgressive "art."

Stockhausen saw it that way. As an artist, he has expanded the sound palette of music with electronics, musique concrète, and aleatory processes; he's tried to introduce the element of theater to music, to widen its spatial and sonic scope. Gruppen (1955-57) is for three orchestras; Carré, for four orchestra and four choruses — attempts to resolve problems of the sound-space-time relationship. His Licht cycle was started in 1977 and, in addition to conventional musicians, calls for actor-musicians and mimes as well as special environments; it is now about 35 hours long. Part of that cycle is the so-called "Helicopter Quartet," which premiered in 1994. The piece realizes a "dream" the composer had: "I heard and saw the four string players in four helicopters flying in the air and playing. At the same time I saw people on the ground seated in an audio-visual hall, others were standing outdoors on a large public plaza. In front of them, four towers of television screens and loudspeakers had been set up: at the left, half-left, half-right, right. At each of the four positions one of the four string players could be heard and seen in close-up. Most of the time, the string players played tremoli which blended so well with the timbres and the rhythms of the rotor blades that the helicopters sounded like musical instruments."

Well, you can see how Stockhausen's effort is dwarfed by the recent catastrophe.

A step-by-step recap of the horrible experience — which unfolded as a series of spectacular surprises — might be useful. On a clear day, a plane hits one of the World Trade Center towers. Was it an accident? A problem with air traffic control? Then a second plane hits the other tower, creating a great mass of fire. Two towering infernos blemish the blue sky, with gaping punctures in their sides. Every fire engine, police car and ambulance is filling the air with sirens as they rush to the emergency site. While all this is going on, a third plane hits the Pentagon. What's next? A plane crashes in Pennsylvania — is it connected? We learn that these airliners-turned-missiles were hijacked. How many more are in the air ready for attack? But never mind for now, because the unthinkable is about to happen: one of the towers collapses within a matter of ten seconds or so. Astonishing formations of billowing smoke shoot into the air and fill the streets of lower Manhattan. Does anything remain behind the smoke? But no, the tower really did crumble. For the moment its twin brother remains standing — fires rage within, and the upper fifth of the building resembles a burnt-out cube. Then the unthinkable happens again: the second tower collapses, and its fall is even more dramatic than the first. A blizzard of debris, dust, and paper shoots out in all directions, black smoke rushing chaotically through the streets with volcanic velocity.

If you were in certain parts of New York and New Jersey at the time of the events, the "live" show was gripping, one of the most visually arresting around. Television, which widened the audience considerably, kept on showing the mesmerizing and jolting images of jets slicing into the towers. New footage appeared regularly, showing different angles of the collisions, explosions, collapse, smoke-play. Shots of people, unwitting performers in a satanic scenario, running away from the falling steel and toxic fumes. A ballet mécanique of madness. In fact, a number of children interviewed said that what they saw on television looked "fake" (which says a lot about how "real" and prevalent Hollywood disaster images are in the culture). Was this really happening?

This was really happening. In several tightly timed fell swoops, by attacking the preeminent symbols of America's commercial and military might, the terrorists undermined our sense of security and impregnability — and, given the phallic nature of the twin towers, effectively castrated a culture (at least momentarily). They outdid Hollywood and reality television, not to mention airport security, air traffic control, the National Guard, the National Security Agency and the CIA. They forever scarred the iconic New York skyline. And, as if that were not enough, they did a tremendous amount of practical damage, psychic and financial damage, turning our economy and stock market topsy-turvy. All of this on top of the catastrophic loss of life.

This was, quite simply, a masterpiece of terrorism. It caught the attention of the world, if not Stockhausen's cosmos. And if the mastermind behind it all is Osama bin Laden — through his indoctrination and training of young men, his suicide strategies, his Mephistophelean plots — he's more of an evil genius than any super-villain in a Bond movie, or anywhere else, for that matter. There's even the possibility that he made a killing in the stock market, investing in so-called "puts" in American and United Airlines right before the crashes, allowing him to sell his stocks at a high price no matter how much they plunged.

And as we learn in the aftermath how these terrorists integrated themselves into American society, taking advantage of its openness and diversity — even receiving their flight training in Florida! — we are dumbstruck by their ruthless focus and determination. None of the 19 hijackers had criminal records. They all dedicated their lives to this one act, put all their eggs in one basket. And for them it paid off. (Ultimately, though, the international response to Bin Laden's deadly activities of 11 September may prove his undoing.)

So one of Stockhausen's points is certainly well taken. This was a masterful operation, a Gesamtkunstwerk of death and destruction. There's no denying that there was method to their madness, and that the spectacle outdid anything Robert Wilson could ever direct. Unfortunately, by his dubbing the terror as a "work of art" — the ultimate Duchampian maneuver — Stockhausen seems to be legitimizing it in some way. But I don't believe for a second that Stockhausen is legitimizing the act: he's not so inhumane or insane. That's not his intention, not at all. But others are, in fact, doing that. For instance, Italian playwright and director Dario Fo was quoted in the Frankfurter Allgemeine Zeitung as saying: "Big speculators joyfully splash about in an economy that lets millions of people die every year in misery. What are 20,000 dead in New York by comparison? Regardless of who carried out the massacre, this violence is the legitimate daughter of the culture of violence, hunger and inhumane exploitation." And there are many others who provide this kind of balance-sheet rationalization. Stockhausen didn't do that. It is also interesting to point out that the magnitude of the catastrophe, an artful work of non-art, has rendered much so-called "art" as irrelevant, worthless, silly or tasteless — not only the "Helicopter Quartet," but Hollywood disaster movies and other kitsch.

Stockhausen, in focusing on the formal and visual elements of the terrorist deathwork, forgot the idea that (as Bach indicated in all of his manuscripts) all art should be created for the greater glory of God — unless, of course, you have some perverted notion of what God is.
