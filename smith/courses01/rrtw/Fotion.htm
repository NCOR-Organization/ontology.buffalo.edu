Fighting a just war

Nick Fotion

The events of 11 September elicited responses of surprise, shock and horror, and, as those who planned them hoped, of terror. Momentarily, these responses blotted out judgment. But very quickly those in government and the people at large in the United States made a judgment about what happened. When they did, their judgment tended to be automatic or intuitive in nature. What happened was felt to be so obviously wrong that little or no thought was required. Wrong is wrong, and that is the end of the matter.

But there are reasons for going beyond our intuitions when making moral judgments. The first is that intuitive judgments aren't always trustworthy. What seems wrong right away might turn out to be less wrong or perhaps more wrong, or not wrong at all, upon reflection. The second reason is that although you and I might be convinced intuitively that what happened on 11 September is very wrong, others might not be. Many in Europe, Asia and Africa did not respond to the attacks in the negative way most Americans did. Some reflection might, then, be useful by way of helping others better understand the significance of what happened. Third, reflection on 11 September can also be useful in suggesting how the US and its allies should respond to these events. Reflection might very well suggest that one way of responding is more or less appropriate than another.

But how should the process of reflection proceed? Fortunately, there is a tradition of thinking about war and war-like events that can help. It is called Just War Theory. This theory evolved in various cultures throughout the world, including Islam, to guide people in their thinking about the horrors of war. Although the theory is not universally accepted, it is recognised widely enough to have credibility as a tool for helping rulers and citizens alike to think more clearly about the ethics of war.

The theory has two parts. The first deals with the right and wrong of getting involved in war (i.e., the justice of the war or jus ad bellum) and the second with the right and wrong of actually fighting in a war once it starts (justice in war or jus in bello). Both parts of the theory establish standards of conduct that must be reached in order to be involved in war justly. These standards can be thought of as hurdles over which a nation or some group must jump before it can say that it is rightly involved in war.

There are six hurdles on the justice-of-the-war side. The most important is called the Just Cause Principle. This principle says that there must be a good reason for going to war. One such reason is that a nation or group is, or has recently been, under attack by an aggressor. Another is that a friendly nation is, or has recently been, under attack. Still another is that a large number of people are being slaughtered. A related reason is that some catastrophe such as a famine has befallen a people, and the government in charge is incapable of doing anything about it.

The second hurdle or standard to be met is the Legitimate Authority Principle. To be just, a war must be started by those leaders in government or the group who are authorised to do so, and not by some maverick general, wild-eyed politician, some private association or group, etc. The third hurdle is the Good Intentions Principle. This can mean many things but recently it has come to mean that the nation or group contemplating war will act on the basis of the Just Cause Principle. Thus if a nation goes to war to help an ally, it should act accordingly and not take advantage of the situation by occupying or exploiting the ally.

The fourth hurdle is the Likelihood of Success Principle. This principle simply says that if there is little or no likelihood of succeeding in a military venture, war should not be started. If no good can come from war, it should not be chosen because war is such a horrible option. A related principle is called Proportionality. This principle says that if there is a strong likelihood that going to war will do much more harm than good, then entering war is wrong. The sixth principle is called Last Resort. This principle makes the reasonable assumption that war brings so much pain and suffering to many people that nations or groups should not enter into it quickly. Instead, all means short of war should be tried before war or some war-like activity is chosen.

The second part of Just War Theory usually takes the form of two principles. The first is Proportionality, once again, only now the principle is taken as applying not to the whole of war but to individual battles or campaigns within war. There are difficulties when it comes to applying this principle. But, roughly, it tells a commander not to choose those options that are likely to cause excessive casualties (to any and all the participants). For example, when taking a hill, a less costly indirect assault might very well be preferred over a direct one. The second principle is called Discrimination. This principle tells those in charge to aim at certain people and objects (such as soldiers, airports and military supply trains) and not other people and objects (such as civilians, hospitals and schools).

One of the key ideas in applying Just War Theory is that each and every Justice-of-the-War principle has to be satisfied before a nation or group can be said to be justly involved in war. A nation could claim that it is justified in going to war since Just Cause, Proper Authorization, Good Intentions, Likelihood of Success and the Proportionality Principle are all on its side. Still, if it entered the war as a first resort, its claim to acting justly fails.

Another key idea in applying the theory is that the two parts must be judged separately. Thus even if a group satisfied all the Justice-of-the-War principles, it would still be acting unjustly if it violated the Principle of Discrimination in the war by killing civilians indiscriminately. Conversely, a nation could enter a war for unjust reasons (by not satisfying one or more of the six principles) and yet, fight a clean war once it started.

In terms of this Just War Theory, what can be said about what happened on 11 September, 2001? Before answering that question we need an account, a brief one, of what actually happened. Four commercial airliners were commandeered by a total of 19 or 20 men. After taking control of the planes, three were deliberately crashed into buildings. These events were reminiscent of the Kamikaze attacks of World War II. Two of the planes hit the World Trade Center main buildings, and one hit the Pentagon. The fourth plane evidently did not reach its target - possibly the White House. About 6,000 people were killed. Most of the dead were in the World Trade Center. However, many died in the Pentagon and, of course, all the attackers as well as all the passengers in the four planes also died. The attacks were unanticipated, unannounced, unacknowledged and, evidently, well coordinated. Other attacks may have been planned, but for one reason or another not carried out.

There are serious problems in applying Just War Theory to these events, not the least of which is that the attackers are not identifiable as acting on behalf of a nation state. Under the cir cumstances it is not clear, for example, how the Principle of Legitimate Authority in the theory should be applied. What sense of legitimacy can those who led the attackers claim to have? The application of the Likelihood of Success Principle is also in doubt. How does anyone assess the meaning of success in these kinds of situations and how are the chances of success to be computed, especially when the goals of the attackers are not well understood? Still, as we will see, enough of the theory remains in place so it can be used to assess the justice of the attackers' actions.

Certainly the Just Cause Principle is still in place. Even if the attacks are viewed not as war, but as major criminal acts, it can still be asked whether what was done was justified. Did the attackers have one or more just cause for acting as they did? Well, the attacking groups were not themselves under direct military attack; nor were the countries that they appointed themselves to represent. From their perspective they may have had strong grievances against the United States. With all its power, America can no doubt be viewed as an intrusive nation economically, politically and culturally. In so far as it is, the claim could be made that US policies and actions 'corrupt' and 'defile' the nations of Islam. Beyond that, it could even be said that the US actually threatens Islam.

But such a threat, if that is what it is, is surely not an act of war. No Islamic nation was being invaded and destroyed by the US prior to 11 September. And no Islamic nation is so helpless that it cannot resist the American intrusions. So this line of thinking hardly constitutes a just cause for starting a war or war-like activities that lead to large-scale death and destruction.

However, another line of thinking more plausibly gives the attackers a just cause. They could claim that they and the countries they represent are at war with Israel and that the US was and is giving direct military aid in the form of equipment and intelligence to that country. They could then argue: "A friend of my enemy is my enemy." This is the same argument that some officials in the US government have been using. They have been saying that the US has a right to attack any nation that directly supported the 11 September attackers or might support future attackers. A friend of my enemy is indeed my enemy.

But, now, did the 11 September attackers satisfy the other conditions of philosophy after 11 September just war theory? One could concede that they had just cause but still argue that they failed to satisfy the other conditions of the theory. Recall that the attackers need to satisfy all the conditions of the Justice-of-the-War portion of the theory before their actions are fully justified.

As we have seen already, most of these criteria are difficult to apply to the 11 September events. However, the Last Resort still finds application. This criterion says that efforts need to be made to avoid war before going to war. Negotiations must be tried, warnings issued, deadlines set, etc. before weapons are employed to settle a dispute. For the attackers, this not only means applying the Last Resort Principle to the US, but also to the nations they claim to represent. Thus before attacking anyone, the Last Resort Principle burdens the attackers to have made serious attempts to convince their own governments to change their ways. For example, they could have tried to convince Saudi Arabia to ask the US forces to leave since they are supposedly 'despoiling' the holiest of the holy lands of Islam. The Last Resort Principle burdens them as well to appeal directly to the US government to adopt a withdrawal policy with regard to Saudi Arabia and, also, other Islamic nations.

Evidence might appear in the future to indicate that the attackers attempted to apply the Last Resort Principle in these ways. But from what we know now, it appears that they did not take this principle seriously. Their attempts to bring about US withdrawal, and to convince the US not to support Israel were half-hearted. They, therefore, on this count alone, failed to meet the standards of the Justice-of-the-War portion of Just War Theory. Their actions were more akin to following a policy of first, rather than last, resort.

Matters get worse for the attackers when we move to the Justice-in-the-War portion of Just War Theory. As it turns out, one of the two principles in this portion of the theory is more relevant to the 11 September events than the other. In this case the relevant principle is Discrimination. Notice the double violation of this principle in the attack on the World Trade Center in New York. The airliners that hit the two buildings were full of civilians who had no truck with the military. They were sacrificed, along with the attackers, as the planes were crashed into the towers. But thou sands of civilians working in the towers were also sacrificed. No doubt, then, these acts represent gross violations of the Discrimination Principle.

In this respect, the attack on the Pentagon was not as morally despicable as the attack on the twin-towers. It was bad enough. Although the Pentagon represents a paradigm case of a military target, the attack is still unjust since it represents a surprise attack (i.e., a violation of the Last Resort Principle). It also, of course, is unjust with respect to the Discrimination Principle since, again, civilians on the plane that crashed into the Pentagon were sacrificed. Had the fourth plane hit the White House it would have had to be assessed in a similar fashion. Although the White House is not a pure military target, enough military planning takes place there so as to make it a legitimate target. But had the White House been destroyed (along with many high officials), the Last Resort Principle would not have been honored. Nor, once again, would the Principle of Discrimination have been honoured since the civilians on the plane would have been sacrificed.

There are, then, ample reasons for saying that the attacks of 11 September were unjust. They were grossly unjust. But, now, what of the justice of a response to these attacks? Briefly this much can be said. The US has Just Cause on its side. It was attacked, many people died, great economic damage was done to the nation and the emotions of Americans were set on edge. What about Last Resort? In one sense the principle has no application for the US. Last Resort is designed to prevent violence. But violence had already been perpetrated. Still, the US could, and should, ask itself whether it has done (or is doing) everything it can to avoid a violent response to violence. The answer seems to be "Yes". The US had taken steps to warn those who helped the attackers that they should expect arrest. Failing that, they should expect to be attacked. The US also warned those nations that harboured the attacking team that they should take steps to keep attacks like those of 11 September from happening again. It has also applied, or is applying, economic and political pressure on those who are associated with the attackers.

What about the other Justice of the War principles? There is no doubt that the actions the US and its allies are planning are properly authorised. The US president, for instance, sought approval for what might be done from Congress, from other countries and from the United Nations. As to the Likelihood of Success Principle this much can be said. There is a good likelihood that the attackers will be harmed in a significant way if they are subjected to concerted military, political and economic pressure.

As to the Proportionality Principle, attempts to harm those who supported the attackers will be costly in lives and money. But presumably the overall good in the form of fewer attacks in the future will override this harm. What about the Good Intentions Principle? Actually, this principle and the Discrimination Principle run in parallel at this point. If the US and its allies mount attacks on those who helped the attackers and/or on clearly identifiable potential attackers, then both principles will be honoured.

The danger is that the response might not be precise enough. It is pretty clear that the vast majority of people in the various nations where attackers train (such as Afghanistan) have nothing to do with these attackers. Thus Just War Theory would condemn a response that indiscriminately harmed the innocent. Actually, Just War Theory recommends an opposite response while, at the same time, condoning a military response that is done at the right time, in the right way against the right (i.e. bad) people. In their great need, those people in countries where attackers train should be helped rather than hurt. Indeed, it would be a brilliant tactical as well as humanitarian move to bomb them with food, blankets and medical supplies rather than with bombs and missiles. Winning their hearts over might very well help prevent another 11 September someplace else.
