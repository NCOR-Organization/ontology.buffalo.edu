As a Taliban Regime Falls Inside Afghanistan, So Do Islamic Convictions Outside Its Borders

By Yaroslav Trofimov

The Wall Street Journal | December 31, 2001

KUWAIT CITY, Kuwait -- America's victory in Afghanistan didn't just wipe out the Taliban experiment to create the world's purest Islamic state. In a ripple effect, it is also rolling back the tide of political Islam in the religion's heartland, the Arabian peninsula.

That is most apparent in Kuwait. Just a few months ago, this oil-rich Persian Gulf emirate, liberated from Iraq in the 1991 Gulf War, seemed on the verge of aligning its criminal code with Islamic law, or sharia. This would have meant replacing jail terms with public amputations for thieves, or death by stoning for adulteresses – as was the practice in Taliban Afghanistan and as is often the case in next-door Saudi Arabia.

With Kuwait's Islamic fundamentalist groups expanding their influence, the emirate's acting ruler, Sheikh Sabah al Ahmad al Sabah, backed the sharia project. But America's swift reaction to the Sept. 11 terror attacks, and the scenes of Afghan joy at abolishing the very same religious restrictions, quickly damped enthusiasm for the measure.

"How can we go in that direction when the rest of the world is heading in the opposite one?" Ahmed Bishara, a Kuwaiti professor who heads a liberal political group, says he asked Sheikh Sabah at a heated meeting last month. Shortly thereafter, on a visit to the U.S., Sheikh Sabah announced he no longer supported the sharia legislation, effectively burying the plan. In recent days, the Kuwaiti government has gone further, shutting down scores of unlicensed branches of Islamic charities, whose vast networks support fundamentalist groups.

"The secular people, they are triumphant now, they feel they are getting power," says Waleed M. al Tabtabai, a leader of Islam's ultraconservative Salafi strain in Kuwait, and deputy head of the Kuwaiti parliament's justice and human-rights committees. "We grieve about the defeat of the Taliban. Our people are depressed."

Until a few months ago, everything seemed to be working for Mr. Tabtabai, who drafted the sharia legislation using a similar proposal long shelved by Egypt. He recounts with pride the Islamists' recent achievements: In 1997, they made government inspectors ensure modest behavior at entertainment events, barring dancing at concerts. Two years ago, they banned entertainment altogether during the holy month of Ramadan.

But things have changed since the Taliban's fall. "Now, the secular people want to abolish all Islamic rules that are applied in Kuwait or Saudi Arabia," Mr. Tabtabai says. "There are even some voices about permitting alcohol."

Some are already openly consuming alcohol. A visiting journalist was invited on Christmas eve to a diwaniya -- an informal club -- attended by dozens of liberal Kuwaiti professors, businessmen, former ministers and parliament members. "Merry Christmas" was the common greeting, as most guests dressed in traditional dishdasha robes, cradled glasses filled with smuggled French wine or Scottish whisky -- technically committing a crime.

Just as heady is the new media debate about how Gulf societies could have produced someone like Osama bin Laden -- and what should be done to effect change. "Time is with the liberals now," says Munira al Fakhro, vice president for the Society of Democratic Action, one of several political groups that have sprung up in recent months in Bahrain.

Many Gulf liberals say U.S. pressure is indispensable to ensure the region's monarchies modernize their societies and marginalize radical Islamists.

"The main thing for the regimes is to stay in power, and so they are using the Islam to suppress the masses," says Shamlan al-Essa, a professor of political science at Kuwait University. "Pressure is the only way; they don't listen to anybody except the Americans."

To be sure, Kuwait is one of the region's more democratic societies. It boasts a powerful parliament that is elected by part of the male population, and its liberals are a political force to be reckoned with. Mr. Bishara's group, the National Democratic Movement, includes scores of influential politicians -- such as the emirate's finance minister and the chairman of the parliament's foreign-affairs committee.

To Mr. Bishara, the Sept. 11 tragedy and the Taliban defeat mark a turning point in Arab history, an event on the scale of Israel's victory in the 1967 Six Day war. Just as that defeat destroyed pan-Arab nationalism, Mr. Bishara wants to ensure current events remove radical Islam as a dominant political force.

The ultimate objective of the reinvigorated liberal movement, Mr. Bishara says, is "to reform Islam the way Martin Luther reformed the Catholic church." To give the movement a fillip, he plans in March to convene a conference in Kuwait of change-minded Islamic scholars from around the world.

Others condemn such ideas. "From their point of view, reformation means dropping Islam," says Tareq M. al Suwaidan, an American-educated leader of the relatively moderate Muslim Brotherhood and host of popular Islamic programs on several Arab satellite TV channels.

That doesn't mean reform isn't needed, says Mr. Suwaidan, who wants the Islamist movement to start advocating women's rights, democracy and freedom of speech, while opening dialogue with the West and distancing itself more clearly from violence. Indeed, under the unlikely name of American Creativity Academy, he founded in 1997 a private school in Kuwait that combines strict Islamic rules with an American curriculum.

"Islam," he says, "doesn't ask you to just teach the Quran and be backward in science and math."
