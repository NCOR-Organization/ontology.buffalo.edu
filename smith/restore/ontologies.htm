Ontology: Philosophical and Computational

Barry Smith

Draft: September 28, 1999
Philosophical Ontology
Ontology as a branch of philosophy is the science of what is, of the kinds and structures of the objects, properties and relations in every area of reality. 'Ontology' in this sense is often used in such a way as to be synonymous with 'metaphysics'. In simple terms it seeks the classification of entities. Each scientific field will of course have its own preferred ontology, defined by the field's vocabulary and by the canonical formulations of its theories. Traditional (philosophical) ontologists have tended to model themselves on these scientific ontologies, either by producing theories which are like scientific theories but radically more general than these, or by producing theories which represent a regimentation of scientific theories or a clarification of their foundations. Philosophical ontologists have more recently begun to concern themselves not only with the world as this is studied by the sciences, but also with domains of practical activity such as law, medicine, engineering, commerce. They seek to apply the tools of philosophical ontology in order to solve problems which arise in these domains.
 

Ontology and Information Systems
In the field of information processing there arises what we might call the Tower of Babel problem. Different groups of data-gatherers have their own idiosyncratic terms and concepts in terms of which they represent the information they receive. When the attempt is made to put this information together, methods must be found to resolve terminological and conceptual incompatibilities. Initially, such incompatibilities were resolved on a case-by-case basis. Gradually, however, it was realized that the provision, once and for all, of a 'concise and unambiguous description of principal, relevant entities of an application domain and their potential relations to each other' (Schulze-Kremer 1997) would provide significant advantages over the case-by-case resolution of incompatibilities. (1)

This is because each group of data-gatherers would then need to perform the task of making its terms and concepts compatible with those of other such groups only once: by calibrating its results in the terms of the single canonical description. The term 'ontology' is used by information scientists to refer precisely to a canonical description of this sort or to an associated classificatory theory. An ontology is a neutral and computationally tractable description or theory of a given domain which can be accepted and reused by all information gatherers in that domain.
 

Uses of Ontology
We can distinguish the following broad areas in which ontological methods are currently being used in the way described: (2)

  • Knowledge engineering, knowledge representation, knowledge management, knowledge sharing, knowledge integration. Consider for example the Knowledge Interchange Format (KIF) developed by Mike Genesereth and associates (Genesereth 1991), and the language developed Ontolingua on the basis of KIF by Tom Gruber and his associates (Gruber 1995). KIF is designed as a framework for the interchange of knowledge among disparate programs and which comprehends modules designed to allow the representation of, for example, mathematical and physical information. The Ontolingua, the KIF translation language, is used as a working tool for the construction of medical ontologies, for example by the Ontology group of the Medical Informatics Unit in Rome.
  • Information retrieval and extraction. Consider for example the problem of internet navigation, a problem which turns not least on the immense diversity of sources from which internet content is derived. Here a common ontology can in principle provide a means to tame this diversity in a way which would involve not only producers but also the consumers of on-line information (Guarino, Masolo and Vetere 1999).
  • Natural language translation. One goal of applied information systems ontology is the provision of an Interlingua, a common target language for natural language translation which would alleviate the need to construct ad hoc translators for each pair of natural languages by constructing for each such language a single translator into the common target. Thus for example the Semantic Networks Research Group (SNeRG) of the University at Buffalo tackles the problem of natural language understanding via the development of a computational agent that can perform common-sense reasoning. (Rapaport, Shapiro and Wiebe, J. M. 1997)
  • Database design, conceptual modeling, information systems design, information systems integration. The Tower of Babel problem arises above all in the realm of database construction. In constructing software tools for merging large databases, it has proved fruitful to develop common ontologies in terms of which divergent bodies of data derived from different sources can be unified together into a single system. In any given field there is characteristically a wide variety of databases using their own categories for storing data objects. Different databases may use identical labels but with different meanings; alternatively the same meaning may be expressed via different names. Here again, ontologies are used to resolve these problems by supplying a single explicit specification of the meanings and relations involved in given application domains. If all databases are capable of being calibrated in terms of a single ontology (a single consistent set of category labels) then the prospect arises of leveraging the thousands of person-years of effort that has been invested in creating separate database resources in such a way as to create, in more or less automatic fashion, integrated knowledge bases of a scale hitherto unimagined. On the other hand, the obstacles standing in the way of the construction of such a single ontology can be prodigious also. Consider the task of establishing a common ontology which would provide a neutral and common framework for all descriptions of historical facts (a common ontology for world history).
  • Enterprise integration. Consider a large international banking corporation with subsidiaries in different countries throughout the world. The corporation seeks to integrate the information systems within its separate parts, to make them intercommunicable. Here again a common ontology is needed in order to provide a common framework of communication, and the provision of such a common ontology is by no means an easy task, given that the objects in the realms of finance, credit, securities, collateral and so on are structured and partitioned in different ways in different cultures. (Smith and Zaibert 1997) Or consider the work of the firm Ontek - short for 'ontological technology' - of Laguna Beach, California, which employs ontologists in the design of software systems to serve as 'white color robots' for the performance of administrative tasks involved in the running of repair shops for large aircraft maintenance. Such systems require ontologies embracing within a single framework not only aircraft parts and functions, but also the raw-materials and processes involved in repair, the times these processes and sub-processes take, job-shop space and equipment, an array of different types of personnel, as well as counterparts of the costs and other economic properties of all of these entities, including costs of transport, storage, and much much more.
  • Military applications. Consider the problems which arise when military units from different armies need to be integrated together into a single force, or when militarily relevant information from different regional and international sources needs to be coded within a single framework for purposes of tactical or strategic planning or of risk evaluation. Consider the problems which arise when cartographic data from different sources needs to be integrated within a single automated system for missile targeting or other purposes.

    Meanings of 'Ontology'
    There is a certain tension between the philosophical understanding of the term 'ontology' and the use of the term that is proliferating through the information-processing world. For the philosopher, ontology is a theoretical enterprise whose content remains the same independently of the language which is used to express it. In the world of information systems, in contrast, an ontology is a software artifact designed with a specific use and computational environment in mind. Thus also it is constituted by a specific vocabulary and programming environment. Moreover, the theoretical concerns of philosopher-ontologists imply that they are attempting, in principle at least, to establish the truth about the domain in question. The concerns of the ontological engineers in the world of information systems, in contrast, lead to a neglect of the standard of truth in favour of other, putatively more practical standards, such as computational tractability. Again, philosopher-ontologists are distinguished from ontological engineers by the fact that the former are concerned with the things themselves (the objects, properties and relations, the states, events and processes) within a given domain; the latter, in contrast, are concerned much rather with languages, descriptions, or concepts, and with software representations constructed in relation to a given domain, or with representations in people's heads.

    A compromise position is provided by recent attempts to use ontological methods derived from information science as a basis for the regimentation of scientific knowledge in fields such as medicine, anatomy, genetics, or pharmacology. The different branches of biology have long been afflicted by nomenclature wars, and different communities of biologists use idiosyncratic terms and concepts for representing biological information. The term 'gene', for example, is associated with different meanings in the major international genomic databases. The construction of ontologies in such fields can force researchers to reconcile their different frameworks by requiring explicit specifications of the meanings of fundamental concepts in a common vocabulary. The methods employed for the construction of this common vocabulary and for the formulation of appropriate translation rules will then be not only those derived from biology proper, and from the software engineering world of information systems, but also recognizably philosophical methods derived from more traditional metaphysical concerns (methods illustrated for example in the literature on the ontology of biological species).
     

    The Closed World Assumption
    Ontology has been used by governments, for example in the design and construction of new computerized systems of land registration in the post-communist countries of Eastern Europe. Andrew Frank has distinguished in this connection two types of ontology in the information systems domain. On the one hand there are ontologies built to represent pre-existing aspects of reality. Such an ontology must reflect observable properties of the relevant portions of reality and there must obtain substantial and systematic correlations between reality and the model of reality which the ontology implies. On the other hand there are administrative information systems where there is no reality other than the one created through the system itself, so that the system is, by definition, correct. Stock examples are provided within the world of banking, 'where the only operations possible are the ones built into the program and there is no attempt to model connections to an independently existing external reality. The definition of a client of a bank is "a person listed in the database of bank clients".' (Frank 1997) In such circumstances the ontologist can take advantage of a closed world assumption (which is required, for example, in the construction of a database query language). Without the closed world assumption, the ontologist's job becomes harder and one obvious consequence of modeling an exterior reality is that the closed world assumption is not valid any more.'
