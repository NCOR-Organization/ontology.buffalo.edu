Center for Legal Ontology

    Preliminary Prospectus by Barry Smith and David Koepsell
     

    BACKGROUND

    In an age of rapid technological progress, legislators, jurists and lawyers must grapple with constantly changing domains of objects. The expansion of trading blocks and treaty organizations, and the concomitant growth in importance of international law have subjected the processes of law-making and enforcement to challenges of increasing intensity. Legal systems have been forced to try to fit such things as genetically engineered life forms, artificial intelligences, "virtual" currencies, DNA fingerprinting, and the Internet into legal schemes which, when initially developed, anticipated no such phenomena.

    Legal schemes need to be continuously updated in light of technological and other innovations. Given the myriad forces at work in constraining legislation, revision is carried out primarily in an ad hoc manner. The resultant legislation is in consequence marked by a general failure to examine or develop categorical schemes, or ontologies, of sufficient generality and robustness to comprehend both old and new varieties of objects. Examples abound of new types of objects with which the law has failed to come to grips in reasoned fashion. Do national boundaries exist on the Internet? Are genetically engineered life forms expressions, which ought to be afforded the protection of intellectual property law? If so, are they expressions of the sort which may be patented, or are they expressions of the sort which may be copyrighted? The answers to such questions must reflect ethical, economic and other considerations. But the questions themselves are ontological: they are questions about how given entities are properly to be categorized, and the task of providing answers to such questions can be aided through the development of legal-ontological theories, theories of intellectual property law, of artefacts, and of biological entities.

    Courts and governments, insurance companies and software publishers have been left to deal with such problems by trial and error, often with much confusion and inconsistency amongst jurisdictions as a result. Such confusion might to some degree be avoided through the development and application of sophisticated techniques of analysis based on interdisciplinary research: it is with the development and application of such techniques that the proposed Center for Legal Ontology will be concerned.
     

    LEGAL ENTITIES

    The importance of legal entities in our everyday lives cannot be exaggerated. Rights, property, obligations, crimes, contracts, patents, debts, insurance policies, parcels of real estate, nation-states, biotopes and similar objects define the scope of accepted behavior in all societies ruled by laws.

    The discipline of applied ontology is a new field, but it has already become recognized as an area of growing importance, and ontological methods have already successfully been applied in the medical and other fields. (See Andrew Frank, "Ontology: A Consumer's Point of View" in the list of references below.) The importance of legal ontology has especially been recognized in Europe, where the problems of conflicting jurisdictions have become ever more pressing with the growth of the European Union. A center for research in ontological engineering in the legal field has been established also in Tilburg in the Netherlands (see Kralingen 1997) and Frank and his group in Vienna (see references below) are using the tools of legal ontology to develop software designed to assist inter alia in legal tracking and cataloguing of real estate titles and registration. These same tools could be used to develop expert systems which could be used in synchronizing laws amongst various nations or in drafting new legislation which might accommodate new types of objects or social practices.
     

    NEW APPROACHES TO THE LAW

    The purpose of the Center for Legal Ontology will be the examination and formulation of ontologies designed to be of service in the formulation, revision and application of laws. Legal ontology embraces every object with which the law is concerned, from bagels to battleships, from acts of will to Acts of Parliament, all of which at some point come to be the objects of legislation. The objects of our study will thus be both legal entities (such as contracts and rights) and also other objects which are the subjects of law-making (such as CD-ROMs, biotopes, broadcast frequencies, nuclear weapons or genetically engineered mice). We believe that new legislation, when guided by ontological analysis, can at least to some degree alleviate the problems associated with the rapid introduction of new technologies and other forms of social change.

    In the present document we emphasize especially the practical implications of legal ontology. The success of John Searle's recent book on The Construction of Social Reality (1996) indicates that the theoretical importance of the field is now beginning to be recognized among philosophers. Investigations of legal ontology are of importance also for the cognitive sciences. For there are not only sophisticated legal ontologies which are embodied in legal codes, but also "naive" legal ontologies which guide our commonsensical understanding of laws, norms and rights. This opens up the possibility of cross-cultural studies of legal ontologies, studies which may be of practical importance for example in the resolution of disputes concerning land rights in borderland areas, where the jurisdictions of conflicting legal ontologies overlap. Smith and David Mark (Buffalo NCGIA) are currently collaborating on a series of projects in the field of geographic ontology (ontology of real estate, ontology of national boundaries, ontologies of naive geography) which are in many ways complementary to the areas of research of the proposed Center.
     

    THE CENTER FOR LEGAL ONTOLOGY

    To encompass the full scope of existing, new and possible entities which can fall within the orbit of the law, the Center for Legal Ontology will need to rely on contributions from a broad range of academic and professional fields. The Center will enlist the cooperation of lawyers, jurists, economists, accountants, geographers, anthropologists, computer scientists, linguists, legislators and philosophers in examining the categorization of existing objects and the development of legal categories for the future.

    This multidisciplinary venture will rely upon the contributions of members of every field of study which depends upon and seeks to shape law-making. International relations, banking, trade and communications networking are fields which face problems which arise from conflicting legal ontologies across international boundaries. When laws are made and treaties are entered into without regard to ontology and to coordination of ontologies, injustice and economic inefficiencies may result.
     

    APPLIED ETHICS AND THE SCIENCE OF SOCIAL INSTITUTIONS

    Recently, applied ethics has emerged as a nexus between academia and the professions. The rise of medical ethics and of business and research ethics has shown the proscriptive power and capacity for public acceptance of the practice of philosophy. We propose that work in applied philosophy should now be extended further, to include the ontological analysis of legally relevant domains of entities. By analyzing existing legal categories from an ontological point of view, philosophers can point the way to a better understanding of the domains of entities with which the law must deal. It may in this way be in a position to point to other sorts of defects in proposed legislation, or to propose remedies or alternative frameworks.
     

    ENCOURAGING TECHNOLOGICAL INNOVATION

    When legal systems fail to adapt to new technologies, or when they attempt to fit new types of objects into existing legal categories that are poorly conceived, economic inefficiencies often result. Litigation slows economic development and the fear of costly litigation keeps potential players out of the marketplace. Corporations which develop new technologies must often wait for the legal system to adapt. Ontological clarification may thus be one important tool by which the degree and the profitability of technological innovation can be increased.
     

    EXAMPLES AND CASE STUDIES

    One domain of entities for which ontological analysis is valuable is that of real property. Although real estate is in dollar terms by far the largest industry in the United States, the various and labyrinthine rules which govern it have never been subject to rigorous analysis from the ontological point of view, in spite of the fact that real estate represents a domain of objects for which contradictory treatments abound among the laws of different states and nations.

    Another example is that of intellectual property. In practice, intangible property usually gets protection from only one form of intellectual property law, but there are many examples of overlapping protection. Mickey Mouse is protected by both Trademark Law and Copyright Law. Databases may be protected by trades secret law and Copyright. Celebrity photos may be covered by both copyright and state privacy laws. Different aspects of a single product may be protected by a copyright, a design patent, and a (utility) patent.

    A third example is the classification of various public or private lands and the assignment of various rights to each. The ad hoc manner in which these categories have been created and addressed has led to numerous battles in courts, and sometimes to literal battles accompanied by violence (as in recent disputes regarding state taxation of Indian businesses in upstate New York). To the degree that systematic analysis and ontological clarification may resolve such problems, lawyers, legislators and jurists may benefit from the work of the proposed Center.
     

    ONTOLOGY AND ARTIFICIAL INTELLIGENCE

    The work of the Center will have relevance also for the design of legal software and for the development of expert systems in the legal field. Detailed ontologies of the human body, of diseases, and of symptoms were a necessary precondition for the design of software in the medical field. Correspondingly detailed ontologies of legal entities can be expected to play a similar role in the development of legal expert systems.
     

    PURPOSES AND GOALS

    The Center for Legal Ontology will bring together diverse groups of academics and those involved practically in the law-making process. It is hoped that a new collaboration between these groups will emerge through the development of a network of researchers trained in applying ontological principles to legal objects of different sorts. The Center will serve to:

    • •examine and guide law-making,
    • •generate cooperation amongst academics, research scientists and software engineers, professionals and legislators,
    • •develop ontologies for new types of objects,
    • •develop expert systems and other software of use to lawyers, legislators and jurists,
    • •engage in consulting with industry, lawyers and legislators,
    • •publish reports regarding legal ontologies,
    • •conduct conferences on legal ontologies,
    • •serve as an informational clearing-house on legal ontologies throughout the world via the development of a web-site,
    • •contribute to innovative interdisciplinary training of specialist lawyers.
    The Center will engage in original research, inquiring into new domains of objects and possible objects, for example in the fields of artificial intelligence and nanotechnology. Academics associated with the Center will expect to be called upon to offer detailed analyses of proposed and existing legislation, through solicited articles, private consulting and even congressional testimony.

    The Center for Legal Ontology will coordinate a network of affiliated academics and professionals who will be solicited to render analyses of particular legislation, of particular legal entities, or of the legal implications of particular technological developments. The Center will be the nexus between this network and the legislators, lawyers and jurists who create and work with laws. The Center will also publish a series of reports, targeted to specific legal issues and new sorts of technological innovation, and will engage in consultancy with other groups. It will work with software companies to develop expert systems, research tools and aids for drafting legislation which will be of use to lawyers and legislators.
     

    INTERIM GOALS

    The authors of this prospectus have initiated contacts with academics, legislators and legal practitioners with a demonstrated interest in the field of legal ontology. In addition they are organizing a major international conference entitled "Applied Ontology: A Farber Conference on Law and Institutions in Society", for which some funding has been raised through the SUNY's Conferences in the Disciplines program and through the UB Department of Philosophy. (See: http://wings.buffalo.edu/philosophy/farber.html) This conference will take place in Buffalo on April 24 and 25, 1998. It will feature a special symposium on Searle's The Construction of Social Reality, with responses by the author. The conference will serve as an opportunity to bring together a core group of interested parties from throughout the world.
