University at Buffalo to offer a fully online graduate degree in ontology

BUFFALO, N.Y. – Search any major employment website and you’re likely to find multiple occurrences of the word “ontology.” But subsequently searching for universities that offer degrees in ontology will return a single result: the University at Buffalo. Beginning with the spring 2026 semester, UB will launch a fully online Master of Science degree (MS) in applied ontology. A sister program at the PhD level will be announced in due course. Both programs are housed within the university’s Department of Philosophy. Both will involved elective courses in Computer Science, Linguistics, Management, and Biomedical Informatics.

About

Ontology is the philosophical study of what exists and how different kinds of entities relate to one another — a line of inquiry that began with Aristotle’s Metaphysics. Aristotle, who established a table of categories for classifying things in the world, can be seen as the world’s first ontologist. His effort to identify and organize the fundamental kinds of being laid the groundwork for later attempts to systematize knowledge. Over time, ontology has become essential to how we represent and structure information across disciplines, from science and medicine to linguistics and artificial intelligence.

What Is Applied Ontology

Applied ontology extends this ancient philosophical project into the digital age. Emerging in the 1970s alongside the growth of large computer systems and data repositories, it provides a framework for integrating diverse forms of data so that they can work together. Whereas Aristotle sought to describe the structure of the world, applied ontology seeks to make data about the world interoperable — allowing independent information systems to communicate effectively through shared concepts and definitions.

The University at Buffalo has been a global leader in this field for more than two decades, uniting philosophers, computer scientists, linguists, and engineers in building ontology-based systems that bring philosophical precision to practical data challenges.
