IDO: The Infectious Disease Ontology

Introduction

The Infectious Disease Ontology (IDO) is a multi-site project designed to support the coordinated collection and representation of data relating to infectious disease. The IDO project was initiated by Lindsay Cowell of Duke University Medical Center and Barry Smith of the University at Buffalo. It is supported by the NIH (National Institute of Allergy and Infectious Diseases), the Burroughs Wellcome Fund, the Canadian Institute of Health Research, the Public Health Agency of Canada, and the European Union.

IDO is a set of controlled vocabularies for the description of infectious disease data at all levels, from molecular biology to epidemiology. It draws resources from the highly successful Gene Ontology [1], and also from other controlled vocabularies in the Open Biomedical Ontologies (OBO) Foundry [2] which have proven useful for addressing the challenges of data integration and analysis by providing common systems of representation which can be shared across institutional and disciplinary boundaries. The creation of such controlled vocabularies helps researchers embarking on new initiatives to collect data about infectious diseases or disease-related phenomena by providing them with a consensus-based vocabulary and logical definitions which they can use in designing their data management resources. Use of IDO tools ensures that they can draw on the lessons learned by other groups of researchers in neighboring domains and also that their data can be compared to and integrated with the data collected by these groups.

The IDO project is the first ontology initiative in the field of infectious disease research. It provides a set of disease-neutral infectious disease terms forming the IDO core, along with a set of disease- and pathogen-specific extensions that are designed to cover all the important types of infectious disease phenomena in such a way as to ensure comparability of data. Terms are provided with formal definitions and connected via formal relations designed to support computational reasoning. For more information refer to http://infectiousdiseaseontology.org.

S.a. Pilot Testing

The IDO ontologies are being pilot tested in the domain of Staphylococcus aureus (S.a.) infection [3], using the data collected by the Duke Staphylococcus aureus Bacteremia Group, led by Vance Fowler. Like most infectious diseases, S.a. is the result of complex, multi-species interactions, with contributing factors including bacterial genetic factors, host non-genetic factors such as health care contact, and host genetic factors. Progress in developing new prevention and treatment measures for S.a. thus depends on the integration and analysis of multiple types of data. While the increasing use of high-throughput technologies provides data highly valuable to understanding S.a. pathogenesis, the resulting quantities and heterogeneity of data create problems for integration and analysis, which the IDO Ontologies are designed to help resolve.

The IDO Consortium

A number of infectious disease research groups have together formed the IDO Consortium, whose members are creating extensions for the following groups of diseases:

Brucellosis (Yongqun He, University of Michigan)

Infective endocarditis (Sivaram Arabandi, Case Western Reserve University)

Influenza (Richard Scheuermann, University of Texas, Southwestern Medical Center; Joanne Luciano, MITRE; Lynn Schriml, University of Maryland; Ryan Brinkman, University of British Columbia; Scott Halperin, Canadian Center for Vaccinology; Melanie Courtot, British Columbia Cancer Agency)

Dengue fever (Saul Lozano-Fuentes, Colorado State University)

Malaria and other vector-borne diseases (Christos (Kitsos) Louis, University of Crete)

Staphylococcus aureus bacteremia (Vance Fowler, Duke University)

Tuberculosis (Carol Dukes-Hamilton, Director, Family Health International)
