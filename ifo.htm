The Institute for Formal Ontology



Proposal for the foundation of an interdisciplinary research group at the University at Buffalo


Barry Smith, Julian Park Professor of Philosophy

Background

The data in computers exists in a bewildering variety of mutually incompatible forms and ever more intense efforts are needed to smooth the process of data integration. The most important such efforts lie in the realm of database standardization, to be achieved through the construction of benchmark taxonomies into which all the classification systems pertinent to a given domain would need to be translated only once. Benchmark taxonomies can ensure that all databases calibrated in their terms would be automatically compatible with each other.


‘Ontology’ is the name given by information scientists to the construction of such benchmark taxonomies. This name was chosen in reflection of the fact that, in building information systems ontologies, one is confronted by issues with which philosophical ontologists have grappled since Aristotle’s day, issues which have once again moved into the center of contemporary philosophy under the heading ‘analytic metaphysics.’


Information systems ontology has implications beyond the domain of data integration. Its methods are used for purposes of information retrieval and extraction from large libraries (for example of medical or scientific literature), and they are currently being applied to the problems of navigation on the Internet in the work of Berners-Lee and others involved in the so-called Semantic Web. Ontological methods are used also as a basis for natural language processing and automatic translation, in enterprise integration, and in a variety of other fields. They can be used also as a means of integrating the results of inquiries in neighboring scientific fields â€“ as for example when inquiries in computational chemistry or structural biology need to be cross-calibrated with the results of inquiries at coarser (for example medical or epidemiological) levels of granularity.


The most significant work in ontology in the United States today falls under the auspices of the Defense Department’s DAML project. This has offshoots

  • in Stanford, where the Protégé-2000 Project is devoted to the construction of domain ontologies for science and medicine
  • in Austin, where the company Cycorp is building a knowledge base of ‘formalized common sense’
  • at MIT, in the work of the Web-Ontology Working Group of the Semantic Web Project.

The work of all of the above, however, is marked by a strongly practical and domain-specific orientation. Work in formal ontology, which is the fundamental theoretical core of information systems ontology, has thus far been centered primarily in Europe. Most important, in this respect, is:

  • Institute for Ontology, Conceptual Modeling, and Knowledge Engineering . This special research unit of the Italian equivalent of the NSF was founded by Nicola Guarino, who is also the instigator of the international conference series FOIS (for Formal Ontology and Information Systems)

  • Ontoweb is a consortium of European universities and businesses working in ontology, which currently has 92 participants

  • Other important centers of ontological research in Europe are to be found in Manchester (under the auspices of the Medical Informatics Group ), and in Turin (where a Center for Theoretical and Applied Ontology is currently in process of being established).

The Institute for Formal Ontology

The proposed institute will be the first of its kind in the United States. Its goals are:

  • to contribute to the theoretical development of formal ontology and its applications
  • to serve as a center for research and teaching in theoretical and applied ontology, collaborating with counterpart institutions in the United States and elsewhere
  • Â· to develop upper-level and domain ontologies for information systems and data management focusing especially on providing the ontological infrastructure for those groups working in domain ontologies within UB, including:
  • David Mark and his associates in the NCGIA, working on spatial and cognitive ontologies
  • James Llinas and the UB Center for Multisource Information Fusion
  • Charles Dement and the Ontek project in enterprise ontology and enterprise engineering
  • those working on ontology-related projects in the medical and bioinformatics fields
The institute will collaborate with the Institute for Formal Ontology and Medical Information Science in the University of Leipzig and through them with other European research centers in ontology. One initial focus will be on the uses of ontology in clinical trial standardization, in tandem with Dr Smith’s current work in Leipzig.

Funding

The institute will aggressively seek both government and corporate funding for its research. The College of Arts and Sciences has agreed to provide financing for two additional faculty lines in the Department of Philosophy to support the work of the institute. This will need to be supplemented by at least one administrative staff position. Further staffing needs will depend in part upon developments in the areas of medical and bio-ontology in Buffalo.
